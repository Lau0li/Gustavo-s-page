Module 1: Introduction to Re-Domestication

Historically, it took over 3,000 years to achieve domestication (Domesticación) of the wild tomato. Recent advances using CRISPR genome editing (Edición genómica CRISPR) have enabled teams in Brazil and China to re-domesticate tomatoes in under three years, producing varieties more nutritious than current ones. CRISPR involves precise DNA modifications, allowing scientists to enhance traits like size and nutrition.

This technology could diversify our food supply by domesticating thousands of the 50,000 edible plants worldwide, as only 15 crops provide 90% of our energy. For example, a U.S. team is domesticating the groundcherry (Cereza de tierra), a tomato relative, to improve food security and disease resistance, such as against rust fungus affecting wheat.

Did You Know? Global food security faces challenges from climate change and population growth. CRISPR could help by creating crops that are drought tolerant (Tolerante a la sequía) and nutrient-rich, ensuring sustainable agriculture.

Module 2: Historical Domestication of Tomatoes

Wild tomatoes, native to the Andes, produce small, pea-sized fruits. Ancient peoples like the Aztecs bred plants with mutations (Mutaciones) to develop larger fruits, a process of domestication (Domesticación) spanning centuries. However, selecting single plants reduced genetic diversity, leading to trade-offs, such as supermarket tomatoes losing flavour.

By comparing modern and wild tomato genomes, scientists identified key mutations. Teams in Brazil and China used CRISPR to reintroduce these changes, preserving desirable traits like flavour while enhancing others, like nutrition.

Did You Know? Other crops, like maize and potatoes, were domesticated in the Andes, showcasing the region’s role in global agriculture. Genetic diversity is crucial for resilience against pests and climate shifts.

Module 3: Modern Genetic Modifications

The Brazilian team, led by Jorg Kudla, made six CRISPR edits, including modifying the FRUIT WEIGHT (Peso del fruto) gene to triple fruit size and the MULTIFLORA (Multiflora) gene to increase tomatoes per truss. They also boosted lycopene (Licopeno) levels, a red pigment with health benefits, to five times that of cultivated tomatoes.

The Chinese team created strains resistant to bacterial spot race (Raza de mancha bacteriana), a disease that devastates yields, and developed salt-tolerant tomatoes with higher vitamin C. These advancements show CRISPR’s potential to enhance both yield and nutrition.

Case Study: Lycopene is linked to reduced heart disease risk. By increasing its levels, re-domesticated tomatoes could improve public health while maintaining taste, described as ‘strong and aromatic’ by Kudla.

Module 4: Groundcherry Domestication

Joyce Van Eck’s U.S. team applied CRISPR to domesticate the groundcherry (Cereza de tierra), also known as goldenberry (Aguaymanto), a tomato relative. Groundcherries are sold in limited U.S. markets but face production challenges due to sprawling growth and fruits dropping when ripe.

Van Eck’s team edited genes to increase fruit size, compact plant growth, and prevent fruit drop, making groundcherry a potential commercial crop. However, high costs for CRISPR licensing and regulatory approval pose barriers.

Did You Know? Other underutilized crops, like quinoa, faced similar challenges but became global staples. Regulatory frameworks for genetically edited crops vary, with the EU imposing stricter rules than the U.S.

Module 5: Future Prospects and Ethical Considerations

CRISPR could domesticate obscure plants like foxtail, oat-grass, and cowpea, potentially creating new staple crops that are drought tolerant (Tolerante a la sequía) or heat-resistant, vital for climate change adaptation, as noted by Caixia Gao.

Jonathan Jones highlights challenges in mainstream adoption due to consumer and farmer skepticism. Ethical concerns include CRISPR’s accessibility, as Kudla notes its ease could lead to unregulated use. Balancing innovation with safety is key.

Case Study: Cowpea, a drought-tolerant African crop, could be enhanced for higher yields, supporting food security in arid regions. Ethical debates focus on ecological impacts and equitable access to technology.
